## ABSTRACT

Lithium-ion (Li-ion) batteries have become prolific in our daily lives, and their adoption in aircraft and electric vehicles can bring significant improvements to our quality of life. These batteries offer superior efficiency compared to alternative energy storage solutions due to their high energy density, powerful capabilities, and minimal self-discharge rates. However, the recurring problem of battery degradation during cyclic use poses a significant challenge for accurately predicting their remaining useful life using traditional and analytical techniques. The nonlinear and unpredictable nature of relationships between battery life factors such as charge/discharge states, voltage history, and temperature further complicates the issue. In this study, we address this challenge by utilizing the Randomized Battery Usage 1: Random Walk dataset provided by NASA to develop a Deep Neural Network (DNN) model that accurately predicts the state of health (SOH) of Li-ion batteries. Through a comparative analysis with previously proposed models including Broad Learning System-Relative Vector Machines (BLS-RVM) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNN), we evaluate the training efficiency, prediction accuracy, and model applicability of our model.

**Keywords:** Lithium-Ion Battery, Remaining Useful Life, State of Health, Deep Neural Network

## I. INTRODUCTION

Lithium-ion batteries have revolutionized the field of energy storage, finding extensive applications in portable electronics, electric vehicles (EVs), and renewable energy systems. With their lightweight design, high energy density, and long cycle life, Lithium-ion batteries have become the preferred choice for portable electronics [5][9]. Beyond consumer electronics, Lithium-ion batteries play a crucial role in the advancement of transportation, with EVs powered by these batteries offering extended driving ranges, cheaper charging costs, and reduced emissions compared to conventional vehicles [7]. In fact, as shown in Figures 1 and 2, the Lithium-ion battery industry is disrupting the entire transportation industry worldwide and has potential to reshape global industries in the decades to come. Lithium-ion batteries are also vital for grid-scale energy storage, facilitating the integration of more sustainable and renewable energy sources for the future [16]. For these reasons, the demand for Li-ion batteries is predicted to be on the rise in the coming years.

Compared to alternative energy storage technologies, Li-ion batteries possess distinct advantages. They outperform thermal energy storage systems in terms of power capabilities and response times, making them suitable for applications requiring rapid energy release or absorption. Until 2014, the NaS (Sodium-Sulfur) battery was the leading option in the electric power sector. However, since then, the battery storage landscape has been gradually shifting from the NaS battery to its counterparts, mainly Li-ion batteries. The initial maximum Li-ion battery capacity is projected to increase rapidly, so it is expected to become the dominant battery storage technology in the future [8]. Furthermore, the high energy density and longer cycle life of lithium-ion batteries enable their utilization in demanding applications such as grid-scale energy storage, portable medical devices, and aerospace systems, where compactness, reliability, and extended operational lifespan are critical factors. Their superior power capabilities and low self-discharge rates make them well-suited for applications that require quick bursts of energy or prolonged standby periods.

Despite its many favorable characteristics, Li-ion batteries come with their fair share of downsides. Namely, these batteries are plagued with issues such as battery degradation, lithium dendrites, and thermal runaway [22]. Overcharging/over discharging will result in considerable formation of dendritic lithium, exothermic decomposition of electrolytes, and heat generation which will ultimately lead to a thermal runaway and cause the battery to short-circuit and burn. In addition, Lithium-ion battery degradation is caused by several factors. The first of these is cycling degradation. Going through cycles of charging and discharging causes the battery to degrade over time. Another factor is calendar aging, where the battery loses capacity over time due to chemical reactions which occur within the cells. Lastly, lithium-ion batteries are sensitive to temperature. When exposed to extreme temperatures, the chemical processes that occur within the cells are accelerated, damaging the battery and reducing the capacity more rapidly. As shown in Figures 3 and 4, the accumulation of these factors causes understanding the structure of the battery system to be very complex and hard to predict [10][20]. Because of these reactions, safety issues of lithium-ion batteries exist as one of the main problems with Li-ion batteries [23]. Furthermore, since the battery plays a vital role in many fields, the consequences of not replacing batteries in time and running their power down are severe when used in safety-critical applications. As a result, accurate, real-time solutions are essential to address the complexities of the battery system in real world applications.

Conventional methods have been utilized to solve this problem in the past. For example, Thomas et al. created an empirical model that predicted the life of Li-ion batteries [21]. Although it performed well under experimental conditions, this and similar models are not applicable to generalized, real-time situations, rather only for specialized instances. Instead, past researchers have found more success when utilizing machine learning approaches. Machine learning models are able to capture the complex correlations and patterns between different parameters in order to create accurate predictions of the state of health of Li-ion batteries. Additionally, by training these models with noisy data, researchers are able to create solutions that are noise-robust. These factors, as well as the fact that they are more time-efficient, highlight the necessity for machine learning solutions for battery life prediction in real-world scenarios.

When it comes to predicting the State of Health (SOH) and Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of lithium-ion batteries, current researchers have experimented with various machine learning architectures to find the most accurate and reliable solutions. Two prominent approaches that have shown considerable success in providing accurate predictions are the Broad Learning System-Relative Vector Machines (BLS-RVM) and Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models [6][8]. The BLS-RVM hybrid algorithm, proposed by Chen et al. [6], is designed to handle complex problems by combining multiple hypothesis functions and utilizing multiple starting points, which helps escape local minima, to find the State of Health values for multiple different Lithium-ion batteries. By considering a diverse range of possible models, BLS-RVM can capture intricate patterns in the data and provide more reliable predictions for the battery's remaining life. On the other hand, LSTM can also model for RUL of Lithium Ion Battery prediction, such as the one used by El-Dalahmeh et al. [22], have gained popularity for their ability to handle time-series data effectively. Time-series data, like battery measurements collected over consecutive time points, requires capturing temporal dependencies. LSTM models achieve this by incorporating memory cells that store past information, allowing them to consider previous outputs while predicting the next one. This capability makes LSTM models well-suited for capturing the dynamic behavior of Li-ion batteries during charge and discharge cycles, making them a valuable tool in predicting battery health.

However, Deep Neural Networks (DNNs), on the other hand, offer a powerful approach to solve complex prediction problems, such as our problem with predicting the Remaining Useful Life (RUL) of lithium-ion batteries. A DNN consists of multiple interconnected layers of artificial neurons, enabling it to learn and represent intricate patterns in the data. The strength of DNNs lies in their ability to handle nonlinear relationships effectively, making them ideal for tasks with complex dependencies and interactions.

## II. METHODS / METHODOLOGY

### 2.1 Approach and Objectives

The core objective of our research project is to develop a robust machine learning model capable of predicting the remaining useful life (RUL) of lithium-ion batteries accurately. As a result, we must explore various machine learning architectures and methodologies using historical battery data. The initial focus of our research project is to devise an effective approach that can reliably predict the state of health of lithium-ion batteries, which would lead us to estimate their remaining useful life.

Thus, we aim to address this goal with three key research questions. The first question to address is can our machine learning model accurately predict the remaining SOH of a given battery based on its previous SOH history. Our model's success in predicting SOH based on historical data would provide valuable insights into the degradation patterns of batteries in practical applications, enabling proactive maintenance and replacement strategies. For instance, in industries relying on batteries for critical operations, such as electric vehicles or renewable energy storage systems, accurate predictions of battery health are vital to optimize performance and prevent unexpected failures. The second goal of our project is to assess the generalization capability of our model across different batteries. We aim to train the model on data from one battery and then evaluate its ability to predict the SOH of a different, unseen battery. By demonstrating that our model can effectively predict SOH for batteries with varying characteristics and usage patterns, we can establish its versatility and reliability, particularly in scenarios where obtaining large amounts of data from individual batteries prove challenging or costly.

Lastly, to verify if our model is effective, we will perform a comprehensive comparative analysis with other leading models in the field. By benchmarking our model against alternative architectures, we can objectively assess its predictive accuracy and error minimization. This comparison is crucial to determine the most suitable and effective architecture for accurately predicting battery SOH.

### 2.2 BLS-RVM: State of Health Prediction

In the field of predicting the Remaining Useful Life, one of the most successful methods for State of Health prediction is the BLS-RVM system, proposed by Chen et al [6]. The Broad Learning System - Relevance Vector Machine is a hybrid algorithm that combines two distinct machine learning techniques: the Broad Learning System (BLS) and the Relevance Vector Machine (RVM). BLS is known for its fast training speed and incremental learning capabilities, making it suitable for scenarios with limited data features but high demands for real-time prediction. On the other hand, RVM, a variant of the Support Vector Machine (SVM), achieves a highly sparse model by identifying relevant data points, thereby significantly reducing computational efforts and allowing uncertainty expression in predictions. As shown in Figure 5, the BLS-RVM system serves as the prediction layer, effectively fusing the outputs of BLS with the Relevance Vector Machine to enhance prediction performance. In the BLS-RVM hybrid algorithm, the BLS performs empirical mode decomposition (EMD) to extract features from lithium-ion battery capacity data. It generates various prediction starting points, producing corresponding prediction data. These predictions are combined into a matrix and fed into RVM as input, which serves as the prediction layer. The hybrid approach addresses single-time prediction randomness and parameter settings, enhancing long-term prediction and generalization capabilities, making it valuable for predicting the State of Health of lithium-ion batteries with higher accuracy and efficiency.

### 2.3 RNN with LSTM cells: State of Health Prediction

The second method selected for comparison is Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) with Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) cells. RNNs are a class of neural networks designed to handle sequential data, making them well-suited for time-series analysis, natural language processing, and other tasks involving sequences [18]. However, traditional RNNs suffer from issues like vanishing or exploding gradients when processing long sequences, leading to difficulties in retaining long-term dependencies. To overcome these limitations, LSTM was introduced as a specialized type of RNN. The key feature of LSTM is its ability to maintain long-term dependencies in the data by introducing a cell state C, which acts as a memory component. This cell state allows LSTM to selectively add or remove information from the memory, enabling it to retain relevant information over extended periods. Consequently, LSTM can better capture patterns and dependencies in sequences, making it a popular choice for various sequential tasks. The LSTM structure consists of three main components: the forget gate, the input gate, and the output gate which regulate the flow of information in and out of the cell state, making LSTM capable of learning to remember or forget information as needed. The LSTM model used in the experiment is depicted in Figure 6. This experiment likely utilizes LSTM for its superior ability to capture long-term dependencies, making it effective for tasks involving time-series data like State of Health prediction. ### 2.4 Experimental Dataset: Insights and Characteristics

Before diving into the details of our dataset analysis, it is essential to understand the characteristics of the dataset we utilized for our study. The dataset we chose to use was titled "Randomized Battery Usage 1: Random Walk," which was sourced from NASA Prognostics Center of Excellence via the NASA Open Data Portal. This dataset forms part of a larger series in which batteries undergo continuous cycling with randomly generated current profiles. To establish reference benchmarks for battery state of health, the batteries also undergo periodic reference charging and discharging cycles after a fixed interval of randomized usage. The specific dataset we employed involved the operation of four 18650 Li-ion batteries, designated as RW9, RW10, RW11, and RW12. These batteries were subjected to a sequence of charging and discharging currents ranging from -4.5A to 4.5A, a process referred to as "random walk" (RW) operation. During each loading period, which lasted 5 minutes, the batteries experienced the random walk cycle. After 1500 of these loading periods, equivalent to approximately 5 days, a series of reference charging and discharging cycles were performed to establish reference benchmarks for battery state health. This dataset's unique characteristics and design make it valuable for studying battery behavior under random operating conditions and serve as an ideal foundation for our RUL prediction investigation.

The dataset includes several recorded parameters for each battery, such as the relative sample time in seconds (referenced to the beginning of the current step), absolute sample time in seconds (referenced to the beginning of the experiment), sample voltage in Volts, sample current in Amperes, sample temperature in degrees Celsius, and the date and time of each step's start. Additionally, the dataset includes a comment field providing valuable descriptions of each step, including the experimental procedure type (charging, discharging, or resting) and other relevant information. These comprehensive parameters offer essential insights into the battery's behavior and operating conditions; however, we don't have a direct metric to determine the State of Health of the battery.

To estimate the SOH of the battery based on the available parameters, we employ mathematical ratios and percentages. In our investigation, we recognized that the maximum voltage capacity could be a valuable metric for evaluating the battery's operating condition. Consequently, we developed a mathematical relationship between the voltage of the battery and its state of health, which is represented as:

$$SOH = \frac{V_i - V_f}{V_0 - V_f}$$

(where $V_i$ = instantaneous voltage, $V_f$ = final voltage, and $V_0$ = initial voltage)

However, a challenge arose in determining the exact final voltage of the battery. To address this, we referred to Tektronix Lithium-Ion battery maintenance guidelines, which indicate that the battery should be replaced when it reaches 80% SOH. Using this information and simplification, our equation took the form where the final voltage occurs when the battery reaches $(0.8)V_i$. By manipulating the dataset and applying this equation, we were able to convert both the predicted voltage and the actual voltage values into corresponding terms of SOH.

To determine the most informative features for training our SOH prediction model, we performed an in-depth analysis of the dataset. As shown in Figure 7, the correlation matrix provides valuable insights into the relationships and inverse relationships between the numerical parameters in the dataset. By examining this matrix, we were able to quantify the degree of proportionality between voltage and other variables, helping us identify the most relevant features for our model. From the analysis, we found that voltage history, current, temperature, absolute time, and step type exhibited the most substantial correlations with the SOH. These parameters were selected as the key inputs to our machine learning model. Furthermore, we utilized the visual model representation of our data parameters and targets, as shown in Figure 8, to gain a comprehensive understanding of the interconnections between the variables. This visual model guided our feature selection process and informed the design of our model architecture. By incorporating these critical features, we gained a comprehensive understanding of the input variables and the target value (predicted voltage) we aim to predict, which will be needed for finding the instantaneous State of Health.

### 2.5 Proposed DNN Framework for Capacity Estimation

The proposed framework for capacity estimation in our research is based on a Deep Neural Network (DNN). DNNs are a class of artificial neural networks with multiple layers of interconnected nodes, allowing them to learn complex patterns and representations from the data. The DNN architecture comprises an input layer that receives the feature data, two or more hidden layers for feature extraction and learning, and an output layer for generating predictions. Each node in a layer is connected to nodes in the previous and subsequent layers, and the connections are assigned weights that adjust during training to optimize the model's performance. By using a non-linear activation function, DNNs can model intricate relationships between inputs and outputs, making them highly effective in tasks like time-series analysis. The figure in Figure 9 illustrates the general working of a DNN, showing how information flows forward through the layers, and the model learns to make predictions by minimizing the prediction error during training. Given the sequential nature of battery data and the complex relationships between the input parameters, we concluded that a DNN is well-suited for our case, enabling accurate capacity estimation and remaining useful life prediction.

To construct our DNN-based capacity estimation model, we performed an 80/20 holdout test/train split on the dataset, using 80% of the data for training and 20% for testing the model's generalization ability. The model architecture consists of two hidden layers with 15 and 10 nodes, respectively, determined through a trial and error process to optimize performance. The activation functions employed in the hidden layers are the ReLU and Sigmoid, which introduce non-linearity to capture the complex relationships in the battery data. The output layer uses a linear activation function to produce continuous predictions for the SOH and possibly the RUL of the batteries. During training, the model updates its weights using the Adam optimizer, an adaptive learning rate optimization algorithm that enhances convergence speed and efficiency. By fine-tuning the model's parameters and architecture, we ensure that our DNN can effectively capture the underlying patterns in the battery data, resulting in accurate voltage capacity estimation and reliable SOH prediction. To quantify and record the error of our model's predictions for improving our model's accuracy, we used the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) loss function. The structure of our DNN architecture is depicted in Figure 10.

## III. RESULTS / ANALYSIS

### A. Approach 1: Capacity Estimation Results

Now, we focus on analyzing the results of predicting the remaining SOH of lithium-ion batteries in the context of the first approach we mentioned at first which was predicting the next SOH values based on their previous SOH history. To evaluate the accuracy of our predictions, we employed the Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE) as our loss function, which measures the average magnitude of errors between the predicted and actual values. The RMSE is expressed in percentage units, aligning with the percentage-based error metrics used in SOH estimation, and can be represented in our application as:

$$RMSE = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left( SOH_{predicted} - SOH'_{actual} \right)^2}$$

During the training process, we conducted several experiments to optimize model performance. We ran the model for 10 epochs to assess convergence behavior and observed that the loss function was converging well before reaching the 10th epoch, indicating that the model had captured the underlying patterns effectively. To balance computational efficiency and model comparability, we decided to train the model for 6 epochs, which demonstrated satisfactory performance. Figure 11 showcases the predicted versus actual voltage results for all four lithium-ion batteries in the context of this approach. These visualizations try to provide insights into the accuracy of our model's voltage predictions and its ability to capture the dynamic behavior of the batteries over time but fail due to the vast number of given data points. However, the test loss versus epochs graph in Figure 12 provides us a quantifiable error value to identify if our model is accurate or not and confirms that the model's predictions improve with each iteration. In addition, we calculated the RMSE values for each battery as 1.61%, 1.64%, 1.62%, and 1.61% respectively with an average of 1.62%. The consistent values of error for each battery indicate that our model does not seem to be overfitting. These values further validate the accuracy and reliability of our predictions in this scenario, laying the foundation for other approaches that we are aiming to solve.

### B. Approach 2: Capacity Estimation Results

Next, we analyze the results of our model in the context of our second approach, where we aim to train the model with the entire voltage history of a given battery to predict the entire voltage history of a different battery. Similar to Approach 1, we utilized the Root Mean Squared Error as our loss function to quantify the accuracy of our predictions. The RMSE is still expressed in percentage units, allowing us to assess the average magnitude of errors between the predicted and actual values. During the training process, we used the historical data of battery RW9 and tested the model's performance on all other batteries (RW10, RW11, and RW12). Figure 13 showcases the predicted versus actual voltage results for all other batteries based on the training done with RW9, providing a visual representation of our consistent and accurate model's performance. Similarly, Figure 14 presents the quantifiable RMSE value on the training data per epoch for a total of 6 iterations.

Once again, we calculated the RMSE values for each battery, now as the entire history of the battery lifespan, as 1.78%, 1.15%, and 1.54% respectively with an average of 1.49%. However, one critical aspect we analyzed was the distribution of underestimations and overestimations in the model's predictions by further computing their average RMSE values which were 0.79% and 0.54% respectively. By examining the average RMSE values when overestimating and underestimating, we observed that the number of underestimations was greater than the number of overestimations. This is crucial in safety-critical applications, as overestimations can lead to catastrophic consequences, whereas underestimations will only compromise cost-effectiveness and performance. By having a greater range of points underestimated, our model demonstrated a safer and more conservative approach, providing a level of confidence in critical systems where safety is of most importance.

### C. Comparison

To validate the effectiveness of our proposed DNN model in Approach 2, we will compare its performance using average RMSE with two alternative models, the BLS-RVM system and RNN with LSTM cells. For the BLS-RVM model, the average RMSE value was found to be 1.55%, while our DNN model achieved an average RMSE value of 1.49%. It also slightly outperformed the RNN with LSTM cells, which had an average RMSE value of 1.61%. To understand the significance of this performance difference, we computed the percent difference between the average RMSE values of our DNN model and the BLS-RVM. Even though a 0.06% difference may not carry much significance, this value should be considered from the boundary of 100% to 80% SOH which should be around a 0.3% difference of the actual state of health. This improvement may seem miniscule, but in the context of battery capacity estimation, even slight reductions in prediction errors can have substantial implications for system optimization, performance, and safety. One of the reasons why we believe our DNN model outperformed the alternatives is due to the approach we adopted in Approach 2 where our model achieved a more comprehensive understanding of the battery's behavior and was better equipped to handle 'outlier' actual voltage values.

## IV. DISCUSSION / CONCLUSION

Utilizing a DNN with tuned parameters, we were able to effectively address our original goal of predicting the SOH and RUL of Li-ion batteries given unseen data. First of all, we were able to create a model that could train on data from a Li-ion battery and produce accurate predictions for the SOH of the rest of the battery. This is significant because it shows that our model could detect patterns in the data that could be applied to the input parameters in the rest of the battery to predict the SOH information.

Additionally, we were able to take this a step further. Although a model that can predict the rest of the SOH of the battery could be useful, it would require training on previous data from the same battery, which is often impractical to obtain in real world scenarios. In order to address this, we trained a second model on data from one battery and applied it to other batteries to test its generalization abilities. This model was able to accurately predict the SOH of the batteries that it had not seen in the training phase, as shown by the relatively low average RMSE value of among the different batteries that we tested the model on. This is significant because it means that we only need to train one robust DNN in order to predict the SOH information for any given Li-ion battery of the same model that we trained and tested our model on. This removes the necessity to train a model for every battery in use, and improves the overall practicality of our solution.

The methods used in this paper were effective in predicting SOH for generalized Li-ion battery data with noise and limited data features. However, there are still some limitations and improvements that could be made for our model. First of all, instead of using trial and error to determine the depth and width of the DNN, a more formal method such as grid search could be utilized to optimize the efficiency and accuracy of the model. Additionally, the purpose of our model is ultimately to provide an accurate prediction for the RUL of Li-ion batteries. However, we are currently operating under the generalization that the battery will break down when the SOH reaches 80%. In the future, prediction algorithms can be made more accurate by somehow determining the SOH at which the battery will become unusable so that operators will have an accurate estimate of how much longer the battery could be used for until it needs to be replaced. Other future work includes training ML models that are compatible with other models of Li-ion batteries, as we only trained our model on 4 batteries of the same model.
